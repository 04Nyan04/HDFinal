Social Identities and Historical Context

  • Late 20th Century U.S.:
    • White identity was often not racialized, with white people perceived as "neutral" or "invisible."
    • Many white individuals were unaware of their racial privilege (Color of Fear).
    • Discussions about race evoked guilt and shame, especially when confronting systemic inequalities (Michael & Bartoli, 2022).
  • Beliefs in Colorblindness and Meritocracy:
    • These beliefs perpetuated ignorance about systemic racial disparities.
  • Racial Fluidity in Brazil:
    • Race is perceived through factors like wealth, status, and appearance (Stuber).
  • Evolving Racial Classifications in the U.S.:
    • Fractional classifications of African ancestry (e.g., "quadroon" and "octoroon") were used before 1930.
    • Housing policies disproportionately favored white families, with 98% of home loans going to them over three decades.

Race and Early American History

  • Slavery and Early America:
    • Slavery began with the farming of tobacco, as enslaved people of color were forcibly taken to work on farms.
    • White farmers often avoided manual labor, exploiting enslaved individuals for efficiency.
    • Following a revolt in Jamestown (1681), laws increasingly restricted the rights of people of color (How America Invented Race).

Social Class and Family Dynamics

  • Income Disparities:
    • Average Asian household income: $87,200.
    • Average Black household income: Less than half of Asian households (Stuber).
  • Social Capital:
    • Defined as "the social ties that exist among people and within communities" (Stuber, p. 238).
  • Familism in Hispanic and Asian Families:
    • Prioritizing family needs over individual desires strengthens bonds.
    • Children raised with familism are more likely to seek and provide help (Stuber, p. 236).

Talking About Race

  • White Families:
    • Often avoid discussing race, considering it a "sensitive" topic.
    • This reluctance can lead to "colorblindness," where children are raised to "not see race" (Stuber, p. 240).
  • Families of Color:
    • Race is often addressed early due to its impact on lived experiences.
    • Among 5-year-olds:
      • 82% of white children preferred playmates of their own race.
      • Only 32% of Black children showed similar preferences (Stuber, p. 240).

Gender and Sexual Orientation

  • Historical Views:
    • Gender and sexual orientation were rigidly assigned at birth and rarely discussed.
  • Modern Perspectives:
    • Sex vs. Gender:
      • Sex: Socially constructed categorization based on biological traits (Suyemoto et al., p. 111).
      • Gender: Related to identity, expression, and societal roles (Suyemoto et al., p. 111).
    • Both are fluid and can be changed through personal identification or medical procedures.

Conclusion

The lived experiences of race, gender, and social class are deeply influenced by historical and cultural contexts. By understanding these dynamics, we can work toward a more equitable and inclusive society.
